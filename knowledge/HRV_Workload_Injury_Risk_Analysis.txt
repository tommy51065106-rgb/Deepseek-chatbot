HRV AND WORKLOAD-INJURY RELATIONSHIP SUMMARY
Source: Williams et al. 2017 (JSSM)

1. CORE EFFICACY FINDING:
- HRV as a Moderator: Heart Rate Variability (HRV) acts as a critical buffer between training workload and injury risk.
- Interaction Effect: High Acute:Chronic Workload Ratios (ACWR) increase injury risk significantly more when paired with low HRV.
- Resilience: Stable or high HRV indicates physiological recovery is keeping pace with external demands, buffering the negative effects of workload spikes.

2. SPECIFIC RISK METRICS:
- High-Risk Zone: A high ACWR combined with a low LnRMSSD (HRV) tertile results in the greatest likelihood of overuse injury in the following week.
- Baseline Tracking: Individualized z-scores are used to determine "low" vs. "normal" HRV, ensuring the data is specific to the athlete's unique physiology.

3. PROTOCOL & MEASUREMENT:
- Metric: Daily resting HRV (specifically LnRMSSD) and a 7-day rolling average.
- Workload Calculation: Session-RPE (Rate of Perceived Exertion) × Duration (minutes).
- ACWR: Ratio of the current week’s workload (acute) to the 4-week average (chronic).

4. BIOLOGICAL MECHANISMS:
- Autonomic Balance: Reflects the ratio of sympathetic (stress) to parasympathetic (recovery) activity.
- Early Warning System: Low HRV serves as a systemic indicator of fatigue and reduced adaptive capacity before physical pain or clinical injury manifests.
- Homeostasis: Stable HRV confirms the body is successfully managing high physical stress levels.

5. IMPLEMENTATION STRATEGY:
- Holistic Analysis: Never view training volume in isolation.
- Risk Threshold: A training spike (ACWR > 1.5) requires immediate caution or load reduction if the 7-day HRV average is trending below the individual baseline.

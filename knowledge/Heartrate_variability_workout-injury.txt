Heart Rate Variability and Workload-Injury Summary
Source context: Williams et al. 2017, Journal of Sports Science and Medicine (JSSM).

Main efficacy finding
HRV acts as a significant moderating factor in the relationship between training workload and injury risk. High ACWR (workload spikes) 
increases injury risk, but this risk is significantly higher when accompanied by low HRV. 
Conversely, stable or high HRV can buffer the negative effects of increased training loads.

Specific finding in this analysis
- High Risk Zone: A "high" ACWR combined with a "low" Ln rMSSD (HRV) tertile results in the greatest likelihood of overuse injury in the subsequent week.
- Protective Buffer: Athletes with "normal" or "high" HRV are more resilient to training load increases, 
 suggesting their physiological recovery systems are keeping pace with external demands.
- Baseline Comparison: The study uses within-individual z-scores to determine what constitutes "low" or "normal" for each specific athlete.

Practical dosage and protocol ranges used in the literature
- Monitoring Tool: Daily resting HRV (specifically Ln rMSSD).
- ACWR Calculation: Ratio of the current week's workload (acute) to the average of the previous 4 weeks (chronic).
- Workload Metric: Session-RPE (Rate of Perceived Exertion) x duration (minutes).
- Measurement Frequency: Daily morning HRV readings to establish a rolling 7-day average.

Likely mechanisms
- Autonomic Nervous System Balance: HRV reflects the balance between sympathetic (fight or flight) and parasympathetic (rest and digest) activity. 
- Somatic Tissue Overload: Low HRV serves as an early indicator of systemic fatigue and reduced adaptive capacity before physical pain or injury manifests.
- Homeostasis: Stable HRV indicates the body is successfully maintaining homeostasis despite high physical stress.

Implementation note
Do not look at training volume in isolation. A high training spike (ACWR > 1.5) should be met with caution, especially if the athlete's 7-day HRV average is trending below their individual baseline.
